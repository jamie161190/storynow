Somewhere between the last yawn and the softest dream, there lived a little girl named Poppy,and tonight, Poppy was still wearing her wellies.

Mummy had tucked her in with a kiss, Daddy had turned off the light, and Bunny was snuggled tight under her chin,but Poppy's bright yellow wellies were still on her feet. Just in case, she always said. You never know when you might need them.

And tonight,tonight, she was very glad she had kept them on.

Because the moment Poppy closed her eyes, something magical began to happen. A shimmer appeared at her window. Then another. And another,until the whole room glittered like the inside of a snow globe filled with starlight.

Poppy sat up. Bunny tucked under one arm. And there, outside her window, floated the most beautiful unicorn she had ever seen. Its mane was the colour of moonlight, and every step it took left a trail of glitter drifting slowly down into the night sky.

"Poppy," the unicorn said softly, "I have been looking for you."

The unicorn's name was Orla, and she had come with a very important mission. Somewhere far above, between the stars, a whole trail of glitter had gone missing from the Milky Way, and only someone brave enough to wear wellies in the sunshine could help find it.

Poppy did not even hesitate. She climbed onto Orla's back, Bunny gripped tight, and they rose up,up,up,into the velvet dark of the night sky.

"Mia is going to absolutely not believe this," Poppy whispered to Bunny.

Up in the sky, the stars were close enough to touch. Poppy reached out and brushed one with her fingertips, and it chimed like a tiny bell. Orla laughed, a warm sound like wind chimes, and galloped faster along the invisible road between the constellations.

They found the missing glitter trail just past the Great Bear,a river of sparkle that had come loose and was drifting gently toward the edge of the sky. Poppy leaned out carefully and, one handful at a time, guided the glitter back where it belonged.

It was the most beautiful thing she had ever done.

When the last sparkle was back in place, the whole sky seemed to glow a little brighter. And then, from somewhere very far below, the tiniest sound floated up. A meow. Long and gentle and very familiar.

Poppy looked down. "Daisy," she breathed.

Her little cat was sitting on the windowsill below, watching the whole sky light up, her tail curled neat around her paws. And Poppy felt a warm pull in her chest,the pull of home, of Bunny, of Mummy and Daddy sleeping just down the hall.

"Time to go back," said Orla quietly. "But Poppy,the sky will always remember what you did tonight."

Orla swooped low and let Poppy slip back through her window, landing soft as a feather on her pillow. Daisy leapt up onto the bed and curled herself into a perfect circle at Poppy's feet, right beside the yellow wellies.

Poppy lay back down and pulled Bunny close. The ceiling above her was still scattered with tiny drifting sparks of glitter, swirling slowly in the dark like the Milky Way come indoors. She watched them until they began to blur.

She left her wellies on. Just in case.

And somewhere above the clouds, a trail of glitter stretched from one end of the night to the other,because Poppy had put it there.

Goodnight, Poppy. Goodnight, Bunny. Goodnight, little cat.

The moment Chase stepped into the Enchanted Forest, something growled at him from behind a very suspicious-looking mushroom,and he was almost certain it was a dinosaur.

Ellis grabbed his arm. "Chase," he hissed. "What was that?"

Chase narrowed his eyes. He had seen every nature documentary ever made about prehistoric animals. He had argued with most of them. And he knew, with absolute certainty, that what was hiding behind that mushroom had made the wrong kind of growl for a Tyrannosaurus rex. "Velociraptor," he said calmly. "And they don't actually roar. That documentary last Tuesday got it completely wrong."

But before they could investigate,a long, dramatic shadow fell across the forest path.

A figure stepped out from behind a tree. He was wearing a cape. He had twirled it once, possibly twice, before arriving. He had a magnificent if slightly ridiculous scowl on his face.

It was Dad.

"AH HA!" said Dad, in the voice of someone who had been practising his villain laugh in the bathroom mirror. "Chase! I have hidden the Cosmic Star Map deep within this enchanted forest, and you will NEVER find it! Mum said dinner is at six, but I said the map comes FIRST! Mwahahahaha!"

He twirled the cape again. A leaf got stuck in it.

Chase turned to Ellis. "This," he said, "is going to be a very long afternoon."

The Cosmic Star Map was no ordinary map. It showed every constellation, every planet, every known asteroid in the solar system, and three that weren't in any book Chase had ever read. He had been building it for months. And Dad, in a moment of cheerful villainy, had hidden it somewhere in these woods.

Bruno the dog was already ahead of them, nose down, tail spinning like a satellite dish picking up a signal.

"Bruno can track the map," Chase told Ellis. "The ink I used has lavender in it. He'll find it in under three minutes."

Ellis looked impressed. "How do you know that?"

"There was a documentary about scent tracking in dogs. They got the statistics wrong, but the underlying science was correct."

Sure enough, Bruno's nose led them straight past three ancient oak trees, over a stream full of water that seemed to flow uphill for no apparent reason, and directly to a hollow log near the oldest tree in the forest,which was wearing a small sign that said, in Dad's handwriting: You will NEVER look here. Definitely not. Signed, The Villain.

Chase reached inside and pulled out the rolled-up Cosmic Star Map.

From behind a nearby tree, there came the sound of a cape being rustled dramatically. Then a defeated sigh. Then: "I forgot the dog knew about lavender."

Chase grinned. Even Dad, when he was being a pantomime villain, couldn't outthink a boy who had argued with every nature documentary ever made and won.

He spread the map out on a flat rock. The stars glittered back at him in the afternoon light. There, in the corner, were the three constellations he had added himself. Constellations that didn't exist yet. Not officially. But Chase was nine years old, and he had time.

Ellis pointed at one. "What's that one called?"

Chase looked at it for a moment. Then at Bruno, now sitting on his feet, panting happily.

"That one's called Bruno," said Chase.

From the trees, Dad made one final dramatic speech about how this wasn't over. Then Mum called that dinner was ready and Dad immediately stopped being a villain and jogged home ahead of them.

Chase rolled up the map carefully and tucked it under his arm. The forest rustled. Somewhere in the branches, something made a sound that was definitely, scientifically, not a roar.

Chase smiled. He knew exactly what it was.

2 children in one story

Isla saw it first.

A flash of grey. A flick of tail. Meoooow! Pepper the cat shot past her feet and was gone. Right into the big, tall forest at the bottom of the garden. Whoooosh!

"Pepper!" called Isla. "Come back!"

But Pepper did not come back.

Oliver was sitting in the mud. He was driving Digby very carefully through a little pile of dirt. Scritch, scritch, scritch. Oliver always had Digby. Everywhere Oliver went, Digby went too.

Oliver looked up. "Where Pepper gone?"

"Into the forest," said Isla. She stood up very straight, the way she always did. "We have to go in. We have to find her."

Oliver stood up. He held Digby tight. "Okay," he said. "Digby come too."

And into the forest they went ...

The trees were very tall. Taller than a house. Taller than anything Isla had ever seen. And they glowed. Soft lights, gold and pink, flickered on every branch. Blink. Blink. Blink.

"Oooooh," said Oliver.

"I know," whispered Isla.

The ground was soft and springy under their feet. And there, right in the middle of the path, were footprints. Big footprints. Enormous footprints.

BOOM. BOOM. BOOM.

Oliver crouched right down. He put his whole hand inside one footprint. His whole hand. It fitted easily.

"Big foot," said Oliver.

Isla looked very, very carefully. "Oliver," she said slowly ... "that is a dinosaur footprint."

Oliver's eyes went enormous. "DINOSAUR?"

"Shhhhh," said Isla. But she was smiling.

And then, from somewhere deep in the trees, they heard it ...

Rumble. Rumble. Rumble.

There were footprints everywhere. Going deeper and deeper into the glowing forest. And right in the middle of them, sitting very still, was Pepper.

Meoooow.

Pepper was staring at something behind a big shining tree. Something that made a very small, very sad little sound.

Eeeeeep.

Isla stepped forward. Oliver held Digby tight. And there, curled up in the roots of the tree, was a baby dinosaur. It had huge, round eyes. It had a tiny little tail. It was no bigger than a dog. And it was crying.

"Oh," said Isla softly. "Oh, you poor little thing."

Oliver took one very careful step forward. Then another. He held Digby out slowly. "Hello," he said. "This Digby."

The baby dinosaur sniffed at Digby. It blinked once. Then, very slowly, it stopped crying.

Oliver smiled the biggest smile in the whole forest. "Digby did it," he breathed.

The baby dinosaur made a soft sound. Prrrrm. Prrrrm. And it pointed, with one tiny claw, deeper into the trees.

"It wants to go home," said Isla. "Its family must be in there somewhere. We have to help." She looked at Oliver. "Are you ready?"

Oliver stood up very tall. He held Digby high. "DIGBY DIG IT," he declared.

And they walked, and they walked, and they walked ...

"I hungry," said Oliver, after a while.

"Oliver," said Isla, "we are in the middle of a dinosaur rescue."

"After?" said Oliver hopefully. "Pasketti?"

"Yes," said Isla firmly. "After. Pasketti."

Oliver nodded. That was good enough for him.

Then Isla stopped. She stopped very suddenly ...

Right across the path was a web. A huge, glittery, shimmering web. It stretched from tree to tree, all the way across. And it was very, very spider-y.

Isla took one step back. Her voice went small. "Spiders," she said.

"Isla scared?" said Oliver.

"A little bit," Isla admitted. And Isla NEVER admitted things like that.

"DIGBY DIG IT!" shouted Oliver.

He dropped to his knees. He scooped with Digby. Scritch scritch scritch. He dug, and he dug, and he dug. A little tunnel appeared, right underneath the web. Just big enough.

"Oliver," said Isla, and her voice was full of something warm, "that is absolutely brilliant."

"Digby brilliant," said Oliver. "And me."

They were through ...

The forest opened wide. And Isla stopped breathing for just a moment.

There was a huge, magical clearing. And in the middle, there was a bridge. Long and thin and glittering, made of pure light. It went up, up, up into the branches above.

Oliver looked at it. Then he looked at Isla. "You can do it," he said simply. "You do dancing."

Isla stood up straight. Arms out. Chin up. She took a deep breath ...

And she danced across the bridge. Step, step, step, step. Light as a feather. Just like ballet class, except the floor was made of light and there were dinosaurs below and her little brother was watching.

Then Pepper did something nobody expected.

She took the baby dinosaur's tiny tail very gently in her teeth. And she marched. Straight toward a cluster of glowing flowers at the edge of the clearing.

Oliver burst out laughing. The real kind, the falling-over kind. "Pepper more bossy than Isla!"

"OLIVER," said Isla. But she was laughing too, and she couldn't stop.

And then the glowing flowers parted ...

Three enormous dinosaurs stood in the golden light. Big, kind eyes. Long, gentle necks. And when the baby dinosaur saw them, it made the biggest sound the forest had ever heard.

PRRRRRRRRM.

Isla felt it in her chest. She looked at Oliver. Oliver looked at Isla.

"We did it," said Oliver.

"We did," said Isla. "You dug. I danced. Pepper bossed the dinosaur."

"Good team," said Oliver. He held Digby up high one last time. "DIGBY DIG IT."

Pepper trotted back and rubbed her head against Oliver's leg. Purrrrrr.

And they walked, and they walked, and they walked ...

... back out into the garden, just as the very last bit of sun turned the sky pink.

Oliver sat down in his mud. He gave Digby an important pat. Isla sat beside him. Pepper curled between them, purring loudly. Everything was still. Everything was warm.

"Oliver," said Isla quietly.

"Yeah?"

"Good digging today."

Oliver thought about this very seriously. Then he said, "Tomorrow ... more dinosaurs?"

Isla looked back at the forest. One tiny light was still glowing, just inside the trees. Blinking slowly ... like a wink.

"I think so," said Isla. "I really think so."

This story was made just for Isla and Oliver.
