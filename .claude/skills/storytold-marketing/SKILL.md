# Storytold: Marketing Strategy and Emotional Positioning

This skill contains everything needed to market Storytold effectively. It works alongside the existing Meta ads, Google ads, creative, and marketing strategy skills by providing the Storytold-specific angles, audiences, emotional hooks, and content concepts those skills need to execute well.

## The Core Marketing Insight

Storytold is not a technology product. It is an emotional product. Nobody buys this because AI is clever. They buy it because hearing their child's name in a bedtime story, read in a warm voice, with a message from Daddy at the start, makes them cry.

Every piece of marketing must sell the feeling, never the technology. The word "AI" should appear nowhere in customer-facing content. The customer does not care how it works. They care how it feels.

## The Preview as Marketing Engine

The preview-before-payment model is not just a sales tool. It is the entire marketing strategy.

When a parent presses play and hears their child's name for the first time, they are not evaluating a product. They are having a moment. That moment is:
- The most powerful sales tool in the funnel (converts before they even see the price)
- The most shareable moment in the customer journey (people will film themselves pressing play)
- The reason reviews do not matter at launch (the customer IS their own review)

User generated content of parents pressing play for the first time is worth more than any ad budget. One genuine reaction video on TikTok could drive thousands of sales with zero spend.

## Primary Audiences

### 1. Bedtime Parents (Repeat Buyers)
**Who:** Parents of children aged 2 to 8. Mainly mums, but dads are a strong secondary.
**When they buy:** Evenings, weekends, especially Sunday nights before the week starts.
**Why they buy:** They want bedtime to feel special. They are tired of reading the same book for the 400th time. They want something that is genuinely about their child.
**Repeat potential:** Highest of any segment. Bedtime happens every night. New stories, new occasions, seasonal themes, subscription potential.
**Targeting:** Parenting pages, CBeebies followers, children's book buyers, baby/toddler groups, school mums pages.

### 2. Journey Parents (Impulse Buyers)
**Who:** Any parent facing a long trip with children.
**When they buy:** The night before or morning of a journey. Airport lounges. Service stations.
**Why they buy:** Desperation for screen-free entertainment. A story with their child's name is more engaging than any iPad app.
**Marketing angle:** "3 hours in a car with the kids? We have got you covered."
**Targeting:** Travel groups, family holiday pages, airport/airline retargeting, half-term and school holiday seasonal pushes.

### 3. Gift Givers (Highest Emotional Charge)
**Who:** Grandparents, godparents, aunties, uncles, divorced parents who want to stay connected. Friends buying for a new baby.
**When they buy:** Birthdays, Christmas, christenings, new baby, back to school, just because.
**Why they buy:** They want to give something that actually means something. Not another toy. Something personal, emotional, memorable.
**Marketing angle:** "A gift they will never forget." "Their name. Their story. From you."
**Targeting:** Gift guides, grandparent communities, "gifts for children who have everything" searches, occasion-based retargeting.

### 4. Teachers and Schools (B2B, Later Phase)
**Who:** Primary school teachers, SEN coordinators, literacy leads, school librarians.
**When they buy:** September (new year), January (new term), assessment periods.
**Why they buy:** Personalised learning stories for struggling pupils. A child who hates maths might love a story where they ARE the maths superhero.
**Marketing angle:** "Every child is the hero of their own learning story."
**Targeting:** Teacher Facebook groups, education conferences, TES marketplace, school procurement channels.

## Emotional Marketing Angles

### The Bedtime Angle
The simplest, most universal hook. Every parent of a young child understands instantly.

**Copy examples:**
- "Bedtime will never be the same."
- "A story where they are the hero. Every single night."
- "Their name. Their friends. Their favourite things. Their bedtime story."

**Creative concept:** Split screen. Left side: a tired parent reading a dog-eared picture book for the hundredth time. Right side: a child's face lighting up hearing their own name in a brand new story. Tagline: "Tonight, it is their turn."

### The Gift Angle
The most commercially powerful angle because gift buyers are less price sensitive and more emotionally driven.

**Copy examples:**
- "The gift they will talk about forever."
- "Not another toy. A story made just for them."
- "From Grandma, with love. A bedtime story in a voice they will never forget."

**Creative concept:** A grandparent wrapping a present. But instead of a box, they are on their phone, filling in the child's details, adding a personal message. Cut to: the child opening an email on Christmas morning, pressing play, hearing Grandma's message before a story about them. Tears. From everyone.

### The Journey Angle
Impulse purchase energy. Urgency. Timing.

**Copy examples:**
- "4 hours in the car. 0 arguments. 1 story."
- "Screen-free. Tear-free. Their story, their journey."
- "The M6 has never felt this short."

**Creative concept:** A family in a car. Kids screaming. Parents stressed. Cut to: same car, ten minutes later. Kids silent, headphones on, gripped. Parent looks in the rearview mirror and smiles. Storytold logo. "Their name. Their adventure."

### The Memorial Voice Angle (Future, When Voice Cloning Returns)
The most powerful marketing angle in the entire product. Handle with extreme care.

**Rules for memorial marketing:**
- NEVER use the words: dead, died, passed, gone, deceased, loss, grief, mourning
- ALWAYS use: "keep their voice alive", "some voices should never be forgotten", "their voice, their words, for every bedtime"
- The ad never explains the product. It makes you feel it.
- The viewer fills in the meaning themselves. That is ten times more powerful.

**Creative concept:** A child tucked in bed. A warm, older voice reading a bedtime story. Camera slowly pulls back. A framed photo on the nightstand of the child with an elderly man. The voice continues. Simple text on screen: "Some voices should never be forgotten." Storytold logo.

**Targeting:** People who follow memorial/legacy pages, 30 to 50 age bracket (likely to have lost a parent but have young children), grief support communities. Approach with total sensitivity.

**This angle goes viral organically.** One real family willing to share their story produces content worth millions in ad spend.

## Content Strategy by Channel

### TikTok and Reels
Film genuine reactions. The moment a child hears their name. The moment the personal message plays. The moment a parent presses play for the first time. These are raw, emotional, unscripted moments that perform better than any produced ad.

Content types:
- Parent reaction: "I just heard my child's bedtime story for the first time..."
- Child reaction: Film the child's face while they listen
- Behind the scenes: "I just filled in the form, watch what happens"
- Before/after: Chaos of bedtime vs. peaceful child listening to their story

### Facebook and Instagram
Longer form emotional storytelling. A parent writes about hearing their child's name, about the personal message, about the moment it felt real. These posts get shared thousands of times.

Content types:
- Testimonial stories (once you have customers)
- "How it works" carousel: 4 slides showing the journey from form to play
- Seasonal hooks: "This Christmas, give them a story about them"
- Gift guide positioning: "The gift under £20 they will actually remember"

### Google (Search and Shopping)
Capture intent. People searching for "personalised children's book", "personalised bedtime story", "unique children's gift", "personalised audiobook for kids".

Focus on:
- Long tail keywords with purchase intent
- Gift-related searches around holidays
- Competitor brand terms (Wonderbly, Hooray Heroes) with differentiation messaging
- "What to do on a long car journey with kids" type content

### Email (Klaviyo)
Post-purchase sequences are critical for repeat business:
1. Delivery email with the story link
2. Day 3: "How did bedtime go?" (collect testimonial)
3. Day 7: "Ready for their next adventure?" (upsell journey or learning)
4. Day 30: "A new month, a new story" (subscription pitch)
5. Seasonal triggers: Birthday reminder, Christmas gift reminder, back to school

## Pricing Psychology

### Why the Price Feels Right
At £19.99 to £27.99, this sits in the "meaningful gift" bracket. It is more than a greeting card (impersonal) but less than a children's book plus audiobook (expensive). The perceived value is far higher than the price because:
- It is personalised (custom always feels premium)
- It is narrated (audio adds perceived production value)
- The personal message is priceless to a parent
- The preview proves it before they pay

### The Preview Eliminates Price Objection
By the time they see the price they have already heard 30 seconds of their child's story. They are emotionally committed. The price is an afterthought. This is why the preview must happen BEFORE any pricing is shown.

### Bundle and Subscription Opportunities
- **Occasion bundles:** Birthday + Christmas + Back to School at a discount
- **Category bundles:** Bedtime + Journey + Learning for one child
- **Monthly subscription:** New story each month, £9.99/mo (lower per-story price, higher LTV)
- **Family plan:** Multiple children on one account

## Launch Strategy

### Phase 1: Soft Launch (First 2 Weeks)
- Share with friends and family for genuine feedback
- Get 5 to 10 real testimonials with permission to share
- Film 3 to 5 reaction videos (parents and children)
- Post organically on personal social media

### Phase 2: Organic Push (Weeks 3 to 4)
- Post reaction videos on TikTok and Reels
- Share testimonial stories on Facebook
- Submit to parenting blogs and gift guides for review
- Post in parenting Facebook groups (value first, not spam)

### Phase 3: Paid (Month 2+)
- Meta ads targeting bedtime parents first (highest repeat potential)
- Retarget website visitors who reached the voice selection screen but did not complete
- Lookalike audiences from first 50 customers
- Google search ads on "personalised children's" terms

### Phase 4: Scale
- Gift targeting ahead of holidays
- Journey targeting ahead of half terms
- Teacher/school outreach for Learning category
- PR push around the memorial voice feature when it launches

## Competitive Positioning

Competitors (Childbook.ai, StoryBee, ReadKidz) are all DIY tools. The parent builds the story themselves. They are platforms, not products.

Storytold is done-for-you. Fill in the form, hear the story. That is a completely different buying experience. "Canva exists. Designers still get paid. Some people want the thing made, not the tool to make it."

**Storytold's moat:**
- Preview before payment (nobody else does this)
- Personal message read aloud (nobody else does this)
- Category-matched narrator voices (nobody else does this)
- Occasion-led product design (nobody else does this)
- The emotional gift positioning (nobody else does this)
